About Me

I am a glaciologist and quantitative geoscientist investigating the critical feedbacks between glaciation, climate, and landscape evolution. Using advanced 3D numerical glacier modeling alongside targeted field observations, my work focuses on building process-based parameterizations to better anticipate changes across Earth's ice sheets and mountain glaciers.

Currently, my research targets the eastern shear margin of Thwaites Glacier, Antarctica, as part of the International Thwaites Glacier Collaboration (ITGC) TIME Project. Previously, I completed my PhD specializing in ice modeling and the structural evolution of debris-covered glacier systems in High Mountain Asia, following six years spent teaching mathematics and physics at an international boarding school in California.

I am dedicated to an open-science framework, ensuring that all modeling pipelines, datasets, and physical parameters are publicly archived to support robust, reproducible environmental science.

Research and outreach featured in: National Geographic • Phys.org • The Copper River Record

Current Research & Focus Areas

Glacier Modeling

Utilizing high-fidelity, full-Stokes three-dimensional models to evaluate ice-stream stability, structural shear margins, and mechanical velocity shifts under changing thermal profiles.

Subglacial Hydrology

Investigating the dynamics of subglacial drainage pathways and groundwater interactions, tracking how internal plumbing shifts alter surface velocities and ice loss.

Open Science

Committing to transparency by deploying open-source scientific workflows, archiving model setups, and translating complex physics into accessible community assets.

🎙️ Ways of Ice Podcast

Glaciers are complex, evolving systems driving global climate futures. Written, produced, and hosted by Ryan M. Strickland, Ways of Ice bridges the gap between technical glaciology and the public. Designed as both an immersive audio journey and an open educational archive, each episode explores the physics, history, and landscapes of the cryosphere.
